# Project brief -- thomasson-sfl-review (Piece 1, for linguists)

## Genesis

Grew out of a reading session on Amie Thomasson's *Rethinking Metaphysics* (OUP 2025) and *Norms and Necessity* (OUP 2020), checked against Brett's projectibility-first, field-relative account of kinds. Two pieces were scoped; this is the linguistics-facing review essay.

## Audience and genre

Linguists. A review essay, book-anchored to *Rethinking Metaphysics* (2025). Mostly exposition plus one sharp caution, because the central fact is genuinely unknown in linguistics.

## The news

Thomasson builds the *positive* program of the book (metaphysics as conceptual engineering) on Halliday's systemic functional linguistics. Part I (chs 1-5) is critique of rival conceptions; Part II (chs 6-10) is the constructive program, and its engine is the SFL account of linguistic function in ch 7 (metafunctions, grammatical metaphor). See `source-verification.md` for counts and her own framing.

## The standpoint (the contribution)

A Huddleston-line reviewer. The Firthian lineage runs Firth -> Halliday -> Huddleston -> Brett (loosely). Huddleston came up through the systemic school and left it for autonomous, distribution-first description (the Luke-to-Halliday's-Anakin move). So Brett meets, in Thomasson, the framework his own tradition departed from, now adopted by a metaphysician. That is a standpoint no one else occupies: enough SFL fluency to see what she borrowed, plus the autonomist's reasons for leaving it.

## The disambiguation that disciplines the argument

Keep three senses of "function" apart (the draft must not blur them):
1. Syntactic function (CGEL): relational role in structure (subject, determiner, head). The category/function wall lives here (determinative = category, determiner = function).
2. Functional in Halliday's sense: grammar organized by metafunctions (ideational, interpersonal, textual); structure realizes communicative purpose.
3. Teleological / proper function (Millikan, Cummins, Price): what a term or discourse does that explains its place. Thomasson's "functions discourse serves" is this.

## Central claim

Halliday was right about the functions, wrong to put them in the syntax. The error is architectural: SFL denies the autonomy of syntax and makes the functions constitutive of the grammar, rather than a separate level the grammar is put to. Thomasson imports this in her *vocabulary* (grammatical metaphor names a category shift as a functional re-construal) but not in her *conclusions*: her deflationary results ride on easy ontology (the trivial inference from "the barn is red" to "there is a property, redness," from *Ontology Made Easy* 2015) plus framework-neutral functional facts (nominalization lets us quantify, generalize, theorize). Where the apparatus would need to do inferential work, she concedes it cannot and retreats to base forms and needs analysis (ch 9). So her good results would survive restatement on an autonomous, distribution-first grammar. Her advertised "new reasons" from SFL for the trivial inferences are really a functional *motivation* for the redundancy, not a new *justification* of the inferences.

## Proposed structure

1. Introduction: the unnoticed news.
2. Thomasson's systemic-functional turn: conceptual engineering, reverse engineering, metafunctions, grammatical metaphor.
3. Functions and their placement: Halliday right about the functions, wrong to locate them in the syntax.
4. Grammatical metaphor and the category/function confound: where the error shows; why her conclusions do not need it; her own half-concession.
5. What linguists should make of it: the autonomy debate now has an external philosophical consumer; the stakes.
6. Conclusion.

## Discipline

Rapoport's rules: credit the functions (and the genuine achievement of the book) before locating the error. Keep semantics/syntax and the three function-senses distinct. Source-ground every quotation; no bib entries from memory.
